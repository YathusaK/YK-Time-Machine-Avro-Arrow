Avro Arrow

Canadas Fastest Plane

1945 - 1947

A.V. Roe, a British aircraft manufacturer, arrived in Canada in 45 to gain opportunity from the remains of Canada’s aviation force from WW2 that were left unemployed. This became the company A.V. Roe(Arvo) Canada
The tensions between the West and the Soviet Union escalated into what is known as the Cold war. This led to Canada further committing themselves towards the Western cause by increasing and improving their military.

Click

1950 - 1954

Intelligence from NATO suggested that North America was at risk of attack from Soviet bombers from the Canadian Arctic and predicted that the attacks could occur as soon as 1954.
Around 1953-54 Avro was commissioned by the Royal Canadian Air Force to design a “all-weather nuclear interceptor” capable of exceeding the speeds and heights of any aircraft at the time. It was believed to be an impossible task.

Click

1955 - 1956

The Arrow went through rigorous testing, due to the fact that computer simulation was not available, wind tunnels were essentially relied on with scaled down models.
The plane itself weighs around 20000 kg, with a length of 15.2 m along the wingspan, granted to be “the world’s first computerized flight control and weapons system. Faster than any jet in its class.” (Chong) In the end, the Arrow can fly close to 2x the speed of sound with an altitude of 530000 ft.

Click

1957 - 1958

On the 4th October of 1957, the first Arrow was revealed to the public, among 12000 spectators. At the same time, the Soviet Union launched the world's first human made satellite. Now fear has shifted to paranoia wondering when an attack could happen from space leading to question the Arrow's purpose.
The newly elected Prime Minister Diefenbaker at the time had problems with the president of Avro and were known to not like each other.

Click

1959

In February of 1959, The Arrow project was canceled by Diefenbaker, over 14000 people lost their jobs and anything that had to do with the aircraft was dismantled and destroyed.
